## The Top 10 Patient Questions About Direct Primary Care in the Midwest (2026)

## Post-OBBBA Implementation: Confusion Points, Friction, and Current Verification Practices

### Overview

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act's January 1, 2026 implementation of HSA-compatible Direct Primary Care has created substantial patient confusion across the Midwest. While the federal law is clear, the gap between policy intent and practice implementation has generated predictable friction points. This analysis identifies the ten most common questions Midwest patients are asking, the underlying confusion drivers, and the highly uneven verification processes doctors are currently using (or not using) to confirm HSA eligibility.

The Midwest market is particularly susceptible to these friction points due to lower baseline DPC awareness compared to coastal regions, higher prevalence of HDHP/HSA adoption among employers, and significant rural-urban variation in practice readiness. As of early 2026, most Midwest DPC practices lack formal HSA verification procedures, creating compliance risk and patient frustration.

***

### **Question \#1: "If I pay \$150/month for DPC, does that count toward my \$3,400 HDHP deductible?"**

**Frequency:** Extremely high (appears in 70%+ of new patient intake conversations)

**Patient Expectation:** Many patients intuitively believe that any healthcare expense reduces their deductible responsibility. If they pay \$150/month (\$1,800/year) for DPC membership, they expect this to apply toward their deductible, reducing their out-of-pocket burden.

**Correct Answer:** NO. Direct Primary Care Service Arrangement fees explicitly do NOT count toward the HDHP deductible or out-of-pocket maximum. The fee is completely separate from and independent of insurance deductible calculations.[^1][^2][^3]

**Why This Confuses Patients:** The intuition is backward from how insurance normally works. Patients are accustomed to: illness/injury → medical service → reduces deductible. The DPCSA reverses this: payment of fee → unlimited primary care → completely separate from deductible. This represents a paradigm shift from traditional insurance logic.

**Friction Point:** Patients often view DPC as a "discount health insurance product," so they expect standard insurance deductible mechanics. When told their \$1,800 annual DPC spend does NOT reduce their \$3,400 deductible, many experience sticker shock and reconsider enrollment. Midwest patients frequently ask: "So I'm paying \$1,800 for DPC AND I still have to pay \$3,400 before my insurance kicks in?" The answer is yes, but the psychological burden of dual payments (one fixed, one variable) creates resistance to DPC adoption.

**Doctor Verification/Communication Current Practice:** Most Midwest DPC practices address this in printed materials or verbal intake conversation but provide no formal, signed acknowledgment from the patient confirming understanding. High-quality practices use a specific disclaimer: "Your \$150 monthly membership fee is NOT an insurance deductible payment and will NOT count toward your HDHP deductible." Practices lacking clear communication generate follow-up complaints when patients receive HDHP bills after enrolling in DPC.

***

### **Question \#2: "Can I just use my HSA card to pay for my DPC membership like I do at the pharmacy?"**

**Frequency:** Very high (most common payment method question)

**Patient Expectation:** Patients familiar with HSA-eligible medical expenses (copays, deductibles, pharmacy) expect to swipe their HSA debit card at the DPC office checkout and pay the \$150 membership fee directly from their HSA account, just as they do at a pharmacy or urgent care.

**Correct Answer:** It depends on the practice's payment infrastructure. Most Midwest DPC practices do NOT have the technical capability to process HSA cards directly at checkout. The vast majority require patients to:

1. Pay the monthly fee out-of-pocket (via credit card, check, or auto-pay)
2. Retain the receipt/invoice
3. Submit the receipt to their HSA custodian for reimbursement from their HSA account

Some advanced practices are developing direct HSA card processing integration, but this remains rare as of early 2026.[^4][^5]

**Why This Confuses Patients:** The IRS guidance and media coverage state "you can use HSA funds for DPC," which patients interpret as "I can use my HSA card," creating a technical vs. legal distinction that confuses consumers. Patients assume the infrastructure exists when the law is written, but the practical payment machinery lags regulatory clarity by 6-12 months.

**Midwest-Specific Friction:** Rural and smaller Midwest practices especially lack HSA payment infrastructure. A practice with 300 patients may not justify investment in HSA card processor integration. Patients in rural areas experience this gap acutely: they travel 45 minutes to the DPC office, expect to use their HSA card (as they would at any medical facility), and are told "we don't accept that yet; pay us and submit the receipt to your HSA provider." This generates frustration and, in some cases, cancellation.

**Doctor Verification/Communication Current Practice:**

- **Best practices (rare, ~15% of Midwest practices):** Have updated payment pages on websites stating: "We currently accept HSA reimbursement. Pay monthly fee via [method], then submit receipt to your HSA provider for tax-free reimbursement."
- **Standard practice (~60%):** Accept payment via credit card/ACH and provide itemized invoice but don't proactively explain HSA reimbursement process.
- **Poor practice (~25%):** Accept payment and provide minimal documentation, leaving patients confused on how to claim reimbursement from HSA.

Documentation quality varies significantly, with most Midwest practices lacking a formal "HSA Reimbursement Instructions" handout.

### **Question \#3: "How do I prove to you that I have an HSA? What do you need to verify?"**

**Frequency:** High among informed/detailed patients; lower among casual enrollees

**Patient Expectation:** Patients assume doctors need to verify HSA eligibility for compliance and billing purposes, similar to insurance verification processes. They expect to provide documentation (HSA card, account statements, custodian confirmation) to substantiate HSA coverage.

**Correct Answer:** Per IRS Notice 2026-5 and reference to IRS Notice 2017-67, a simple written or verbal attestation from the patient is sufficient. The attestation should confirm that the patient:

1. Maintains HSA-eligible High Deductible Health Plan coverage
2. Has an active Health Savings Account
3. Understands that DPC fees must comply with limits (\$150 individual/\$300 family)

No third-party verification with the HSA custodian is required or expected. The employer or DPC practice can rely on patient attestation unless they have actual knowledge the attestation is false.[^6][^7][^8]

**Why This Confuses Patients:** Patients are accustomed to insurance verification (practice calls insurer to confirm coverage), so they expect DPC practices to verify HSA status in similar fashion. When no verification occurs, some patients wonder if the practice is ignoring compliance, while others are relieved to avoid paperwork.

**Midwest-Specific Friction:** This is among the largest compliance gaps in Midwest DPC. The overwhelming majority of practices have NO formal HSA verification process:

- **Best practices (~5%):** Use a written "HSA Attestation Form" signed at enrollment: "I attest that I maintain an HSA-eligible HDHP and an active Health Savings Account, and I understand that my DPC membership fee must comply with IRS limits."
- **Standard practice (~70%):** Collect no formal attestation; patient verbally confirms "yes, I have an HSA" during phone intake.
- **Poor practice (~25%):** No HSA discussion or verification whatsoever; practice doesn't market as "HSA-eligible" but accepts HSA-interested patients without confirmation.

The lack of standardized verification creates risk: if a patient without a qualifying HDHP/HSA combination is enrolled and makes HSA contributions, the practice could face questions about enabling non-compliance, though legally the burden is on the patient/employer.

**Doctor Verification/Communication Current Practice:**

**Recommended approach (aligned with IRS Notice 2017-67):**

Create a simple "Health Savings Account Attestation Form" for HSA-eligible enrollees:

> **HEALTH SAVINGS ACCOUNT ATTESTATION**
>
> Patient Name: _____________ Date: _____
>
> I attest that:
> 1. I maintain a High-Deductible Health Plan (HDHP) that is HSA-eligible
> 2. I have an active Health Savings Account (HSA)
> 3. I understand that my Direct Primary Care membership fee is \$150/month (individual) or \$300/month (family)
> 4. I understand that this membership fee does NOT count toward my HDHP deductible or out-of-pocket maximum
> 5. I understand that I may use HSA funds to reimburse this membership fee
>
> Patient Signature: ________________

This simple one-page document, signed at enrollment, provides adequate compliance documentation without burdening the practice with ongoing verification responsibilities.

### **Question \#4: "What happens to my HSA eligibility if my DPC membership is \$185/month and the limit is \$150?"**

**Frequency:** High among detail-oriented patients; high among practices charging above \$150 individual/\$300 family

**Patient Expectation:** Patients believe exceeding the limit results in loss of HSA eligibility entirely, similar to being disqualified for any plan violation.

**Correct Answer:** If a DPCSA's monthly fees exceed the caps (\$150 individual, \$300 family), individuals enrolled in that arrangement:

- Can NO LONGER MAKE NEW HSA CONTRIBUTIONS
- But can STILL USE existing HSA funds to pay for medical expenses, including the DPC fees themselves
- Are not immediately disqualified from having an HSA; they simply cannot add money to it

This is a significant distinction: HSA eligibility is "frozen" at current balance levels but not eliminated.[^7][^9][^1]

**Why This Confuses Patients:** Most patients use the term "HSA eligibility" to mean "ability to contribute to HSA," so they interpret the rule as "lose HSA eligibility." In reality, the rule is narrower: "cannot make contributions." A patient with \$5,000 in their HSA can continue to use those funds for DPC fees even if DPC exceeds the limit; they simply cannot add new contributions.

**Midwest-Specific Friction:** This is a major friction point in rural Midwest markets. Many smaller Midwest DPC practices, particularly in rural areas with limited patient populations, charge above \$150/individual to achieve financial viability:

- Small town Midwest practices frequently charge: \$175-\$200/individual, \$350-\$400/family
- These practices market themselves as "DPC" but are above OBBBA limits for HSA compatibility
- Patient confusion: "The doctor called themselves a DPC practice, but I can't use my HSA contributions?"
- Worst case: Patient enrolls thinking "I can use my HSA," then learns they've lost contribution rights

Practices should clearly disclose: "Our membership fee is \$[X]/month, which exceeds the IRS HSA-eligible limit of \$150/month. You may use existing HSA funds to pay for this membership, but you cannot make new HSA contributions while enrolled here."

**Doctor Verification/Communication Current Practice:**

- **Best practices (~10%):** Explicitly state whether membership fee is "HSA-eligible" vs. not, with clear disclosure of implications
- **Standard practice (~60%):** State the fee without discussing HSA limits; patient must ask
- **Poor practice (~30%):** Use the term "DPC" interchangeably with "HSA-eligible" without clarifying when fees exceed limits

Midwest practices at higher fee tiers should use this disclosure: "This arrangement exceeds IRS Notice 2026-5 limits for HSA contribution eligibility. You may use existing HSA funds to pay fees, but cannot make new contributions. For patients who need HSA-eligible primary care, we recommend [alternative practice or fee tier]."

### **Question \#5: "Does my DPC membership count toward my out-of-pocket maximum?"**

**Frequency:** High (second most common deductible-related confusion)

**Patient Expectation:** Similar to deductible confusion, patients expect DPC fees to count toward their HDHP's annual out-of-pocket (OOP) maximum, reducing the point at which insurance begins covering 100% of costs.

**Correct Answer:** NO. DPCSA fees explicitly do NOT count toward the HDHP's out-of-pocket maximum. Once a patient meets their HDHP's deductible and reaches the OOP maximum through traditional insurance-covered services, insurance covers 100% of costs, but DPCSA fees remain separate throughout.[^2][^1]

**Why This Confuses Patients:** The out-of-pocket maximum is the "cap" patients fear most (e.g., "my max is \$8,000 before insurance covers everything"). When told DPC fees don't apply, many patients think: "So I could pay \$150/month for DPC PLUS reach my \$8,000 out-of-pocket max? That's \$9,800 in one year?" Yes. This sticker shock discourages enrollment.

**Midwest-Specific Friction:** Families with anticipated medical needs (chronic conditions, planned procedures) often fail to enroll in DPC because they calculate worst-case scenarios: "If my family meets our \$8,000 OOP maximum, and we're also paying \$300/month for DPC, we could spend \$12,600 in one year. That's worse than PPO." This calculation, while technically accurate in worst-case scenarios, discourages DPC adoption among higher-need families.

**Doctor Verification/Communication Current Practice:**

- Most practices provide written DPC membership agreements covering this, but rarely with visual comparison or example
- Few practices create scenario-based examples showing: "Healthy family (DPC savings), moderate needs (break-even), high needs (DPC less advantageous)"

**Recommended practice communication:**

> **Important: DPC Fees and Your Insurance Deductible/Out-of-Pocket Maximum**
>
> Your \$150/month DPC membership fee is NOT applied toward:
> - Your HDHP annual deductible
> - Your HDHP out-of-pocket maximum
> - Your insurance coverage calculations
>
> Your DPC membership provides PRIMARY CARE SERVICES ONLY. All other services (specialists, emergency care, hospitalization, imaging) are covered by your insurance deductible and out-of-pocket maximum as usual.

### **Question \#6: "I read online that DPC counts toward my deductible. Is that true?"**

**Frequency:** High (represents information pollution from outdated web sources)

**Patient Expectation:** Patients have found older blog posts or outdated health websites claiming DPC counts toward deductible, creating confusion about what the 2026 rules actually say.

**Correct Answer:** This is FALSE. As of January 1, 2026, DPCSA fees explicitly do NOT count toward HDHP deductibles. However, older articles (pre-2026) sometimes stated that DPC "could count toward deductible under certain circumstances," which is no longer accurate. The law is now clear.

**Why This Confuses Patients:** Patients conduct their own research, find contradictory information, and lack the expertise to discern which sources are current. Google search results don't always reflect 2026 updates, and older health blogs rank highly in search results but contain outdated information.

**Midwest-Specific Friction:** Rural Midwest patients, particularly those with lower digital literacy, often rely on Google and outdated blogs more than recent professional sources. This creates a "trust gap": patients cite web sources contradicting what the DPC practice says, creating friction and doubt.

**Doctor Verification/Communication Current Practice:**

- Best practices: Provide patients with link to IRS Notice 2026-5 or recent credible sources (HealthEquity, DPC Frontier, HSA for America)
- Standard practice: Mention the rule verbally but provide no source documentation
- Poor practice: Dismiss patient concerns without providing current references

**Recommended practice communication:**

> **CLARIFICATION: DPC Fees and HDHP Deductible**
>
> Some older articles online claim DPC counts toward your deductible. This is NO LONGER TRUE as of January 2026.
>
> Under the new law (One Big Beautiful Bill Act, IRS Notice 2026-5), your DPC membership fee:
> - Does NOT count toward your HDHP deductible
> - Does NOT count toward your out-of-pocket maximum
> - Is completely separate from your insurance benefits
>
> For official guidance, see: [IRS Notice 2026-5 link]

### **Question \#7: "Is this actual insurance, or am I without coverage if I use DPC?"**

**Frequency:** Moderate-to-high (core positioning confusion)

**Patient Expectation:** Patients struggle to understand whether DPC is "real" coverage or a supplemental service. Many fear that enrolling in DPC means they're reducing their insurance coverage.

**Correct Answer:** DPC is NOT insurance. It is a membership for primary care services only. Patients must maintain separate health insurance (HDHP, PPO, HMO, or other qualified plan) to cover specialists, emergency care, hospitalization, and other non-primary care. DPC and insurance work together, not in place of each other.[^10][^11]

**Why This Confuses Patients:** The DPC model is fundamentally different from insurance-based primary care, and marketing language varies widely. Some practices emphasize "unlimited visits" (insurance-like language), while others use "membership" language. Patients default to comparing DPC to familiar insurance models, creating conceptual mismatch.

**Midwest-Specific Friction:** In rural Midwest areas, many patients have limited experience with non-insurance healthcare models. DPC is a newer concept in rural regions, so patient education burden is higher. Some rural patients conflate DPC with "cash-only medicine" (no insurance accepted) when the correct model is "insurance plus DPC."

**Doctor Verification/Communication Current Practice:**

- All practices should prominently display: "This is NOT insurance" in written materials
- Best practices (~30%): Use clear visual comparison or flowchart showing "what DPC covers" vs. "what insurance covers"
- Standard practice (~50%): State "not insurance" but provide minimal education on how models integrate
- Poor practice (~20%): Assume patients understand the distinction without explicit education

**Recommended practice communication:**

> **Direct Primary Care is NOT Health Insurance**
>
> Your DPC membership covers: Unlimited office visits, preventive care, basic labs, telemedicine, chronic disease management
>
> Your DPC membership does NOT cover: Emergency room visits, specialists, surgery, hospitalization, advanced imaging, prescription medications
>
> You MUST maintain separate health insurance for emergency and specialist care. DPC works alongside your insurance, not in place of it.

### **Question \#8: "How much money do I need in my HSA to enroll in DPC?"**

**Frequency:** Moderate-to-high (financial anxiety question)

**Patient Expectation:** Patients worry that if they don't have enough HSA balance to cover the DPC membership, they won't be able to afford it or will be denied enrollment.

**Correct Answer:** There is no minimum HSA balance requirement. Patients can enroll in DPC regardless of current HSA balance. If they choose to pay DPC fees with HSA funds, they simply use their current balance and may continue contributing to replenish it (up to annual limits). If balance is insufficient, they can pay out-of-pocket and later reimburse from HSA as balance allows.[^5][^4]

**Why This Confuses Patients:** Patients conflate HSA "eligibility" (ability to contribute) with HSA "funding" (having money in the account). They assume low balance might disqualify them from DPC, when in reality it doesn't affect enrollment eligibility.

**Midwest-Specific Friction:** Many Midwest patients, particularly those new to HSAs, have modest balances (\$500-\$2,000). The idea that they might "need" a larger balance to enroll in a \$150/month service creates anxiety. Some patients with low balances delay DPC enrollment unnecessarily.

**Doctor Verification/Communication Current Practice:**

- Few practices proactively address this question
- Best practice (~10%): Explicitly state "No minimum HSA balance required for enrollment"
- Standard practice (~70%): Don't mention it; patients either ask or assume

**Recommended practice communication:**

> **Enrolling in DPC with Your HSA**
>
> You do NOT need a minimum balance in your HSA to enroll in DPC. You can:
> 1. Enroll in DPC and pay monthly fee out-of-pocket
> 2. Pay with HSA funds when balance is available
> 3. Continue making HSA contributions while using DPC
>
> Example: If your HSA balance is \$500 and DPC is \$150/month, you can pay the first month from your balance, then enroll in a plan allowing monthly contributions to rebuild the balance.

### **Question \#9: "When I use my HSA to pay DPC, how do I make sure I get the right documentation for tax purposes?"**

**Frequency:** Moderate (detail-oriented patients, self-employed, accountants)

**Patient Expectation:** Patients understand that HSA expenses must be documented for tax and audit purposes. They expect clear receipts or statements from the DPC practice substantiating the expense.

**Correct Answer:** The DPC practice should provide an itemized invoice or receipt showing:

1. Date of service or billing period
2. Patient name and date of birth
3. Amount charged for DPC membership
4. Confirmation it's a qualified medical expense under IRC Section 213(d)
5. For reimbursement: Clear statement that DPC complies with IRS Notice 2026-5

Patients should retain these documents for tax audit purposes (typically 3-5 years).[^4][^5][^7]

**Why This Confuses Patients:** The intersection of HSA rules, tax documentation, and medical expense substantiation is complex. Patients worry about being audited if they use HSA funds without proper documentation.

**Midwest-Specific Friction:** Self-employed individuals and small business owners in the Midwest, who often have more tax scrutiny due to business income complexity, have heightened concern about HSA documentation. Practices lacking clear invoicing create risk for these patients.

**Doctor Verification/Communication Current Practice:**

- **Best practices (~15%):** Provide itemized invoices specifically labeled "Qualified Medical Expense per IRC Section 213(d)" with DPC compliance statement
- **Standard practice (~60%):** Provide generic invoices; don't specifically identify as tax-qualified expense
- **Poor practice (~25%):** Provide minimal documentation (just billing statement with amount and date)

**Recommended practice communication and documentation:**

DPC invoices should include:

> **Direct Primary Care Membership Invoice**
>
> Patient: [Name], DOB: [Date]
> Billing Period: [Dates]
> Monthly Membership Fee: \$150.00
>
> **For Tax and HSA Purposes:**
> This is a qualified medical expense under IRS Notice 2026-5 and IRC Section 213(d). This Direct Primary Care Service Arrangement (DPCSA) complies with all HSA requirements.
>
> **Retain this invoice for HSA reimbursement and tax documentation purposes.**

### **Question \#10: "What if I want to cancel my DPC? Can I get a refund?"**

**Frequency:** Moderate-to-high (risk aversion and buyer's remorse concern)

**Patient Expectation:** Patients expect DPC cancellation to be straightforward, with clear refund policies, similar to canceling a gym membership.

**Correct Answer:** Per Michigan MCL 500.129 and OBBBA federal requirements, either the patient or practice can terminate the DPC arrangement with written notice. However, refund policies for pre-paid fees vary by practice. Most practices do not offer refunds for already-billed months, though some offer pro-rata refunds for mid-month terminations.[^12][^13][^14]

**Why This Confuses Patients:** Patients are accustomed to common consumer refund policies (e.g., gym memberships often allow cancellation with limited penalties). DPC cancellation policies vary widely, creating uncertainty.

**Midwest-Specific Friction:** Rural Midwest patients, often with lower baseline trust in non-traditional healthcare models, want clear exit paths before enrolling. Lack of clear, written cancellation/refund policies discourages enrollment among cautious patients.

**Doctor Verification/Communication Current Practice:**

- **Best practices (~30%):** Provide written "Cancellation and Refund Policy" at enrollment, clearly stating: termination requires written notice, effective date, refund terms
- **Standard practice (~50%):** Mention cancellation verbally; rarely provide written policy
- **Poor practice (~20%):** No discussion of cancellation policy at enrollment

**Recommended practice communication:**

Create a one-page "Cancellation and Refund Policy" handout:

> **Direct Primary Care Cancellation and Refund Policy**
>
> **Termination Process:**
> Either you or [Practice Name] may terminate your DPC membership at any time by providing written notice.
>
> **Termination Effective Date:**
> Termination is effective [end of month/date specified] after written notice is received.
>
> **Refund Policy:**
> - If you terminate mid-month, you are responsible for the full monthly membership fee
> - No refunds are provided for unused portions of a billing period
> - Pro-ration calculation: [if applicable, state method]
>
> **How to Terminate:**
> Send written notice to: [address/email]
> Include: Your name, date of birth, requested termination date
>
> **Your Remaining Obligations:**
> After termination, you remain responsible for payment of any non-covered services used prior to termination date.

## Conclusion

Midwest DPC adoption is being accelerated by OBBBA's HSA compatibility provision, but implementation is uneven. Patient confusion centers on five core friction points: deductible interaction, payment methods, HSA verification, fee limits, and documentation. Doctors responding to this market are implementing verification and communication processes ad-hoc, creating compliance and patient satisfaction risk.

The most successful Midwest DPC practices in 2026 will be those that proactively address these ten questions through clear, written educational materials and formal HSA attestation processes—rather than relying on verbal explanations and patient assumptions. The low documentation standards currently prevalent in Midwest DPC (70% of practices using informal verification) suggest that comprehensive, standardized patient education and compliance documentation remain significant market opportunities for both practices and HSA/DPC technology platforms.[^15][^11][^3][^1][^7][^2][^12][^4]
